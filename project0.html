Resilient Economic Dispatch for Thermal-Led Microgrids

Copula-Based Scenarios • Symmetric Heat Penalties • Robust (Minimax) Day-Ahead MILP

Gaussian Copula Symmetric Thermal Penalty BESS Degradation 24-hour Day-Ahead Robust / Worst-case

Project Overview

This project develops a robust day-ahead scheduling framework for thermal-led CHP microgrids in cold climates, where electric and thermal demands are tightly coupled and uncertain. The method combines Gaussian-copula joint scenario generation with symmetric thermal penalties to avoid unrealistic dispatch behavior and improve resilience.

Abstract (Paper Summary)

CHP microgrids in cold regions must meet tightly coupled electric and thermal demands under uncertainty. Many dispatch models ignore electric–thermal dependence or penalize only unmet heat (which can encourage overproduction). This work introduces:

  • Gaussian-copula scenario generation capturing same-hour E↔T correlation and AR(1) temporal persistence
  • Symmetric heat penalties that cost both under- and over-production to eliminate “run-hot” bias
  • A thermal-led MILP with CHP coupling, BESS exclusivity, degradation costs, and charge-first surplus under zero export credit

Key Contributions

  • Dependence-aware uncertainty modeling: Hourly electric–thermal dependence modeled using Kendall’s τ, preserving same-hour correlation and temporal structure.
  • Thermal realism: Two-sided slack variables cost both unmet and surplus heat, preventing intentional over-heating.
  • Operational constraints for daily use: CHP limits, BESS SOC + charge/discharge exclusivity, grid import/export gating, and a robust (minimax) objective.

Methodology

1) Gaussian-Copula Joint Scenario Generation

Electric and thermal loads are transformed using probability integral transforms (PIT), mapped to latent Gaussian variables, and sampled with a structured correlation matrix that preserves same-hour correlation and AR(1) persistence. Scenarios are then mapped back to the original marginals to create realistic E–T trajectories.

2) Symmetric Thermal Penalties

Thermal adequacy is maintained using bidirectional slack variables representing unmet heat and excess heat. The objective assigns costs to both, preventing “run-hot” behavior and keeping the schedule feasible under uncertainty.

3) Thermal-Led MILP Dispatch

  • CHP coupling: Electric and thermal outputs tied to fuel input through fixed efficiencies
  • BESS constraints: SOC recursion, bounds, and charge/discharge exclusivity
  • Battery degradation: Convex piecewise throughput proxy included in the cost
  • Grid gating: Import/export mutually exclusive; export credit set to zero
  • Robust objective: Minimize worst-case (minimax) cost across scenarios

Data Processing & Optimization

  • NREL building load profiles for Tok, Alaska resampled to hourly resolution
  • Hourly marginal distributions calibrated (P10–P50–P90) for electric and thermal loads
  • Scenario engine sampled 1,000 electric–thermal trajectories
  • 24-hour day-ahead MILP implemented in Pyomo and solved using CBC

Results & Key Findings

Compared to an independence-based approach, copula-based scheduling improved robustness and reduced operational stress. On the winter test day, the proposed method achieved:

  • Worst-case cost: −8.1%
  • Total grid import: −2.9%
  • Battery wear (degradation cost): −29.1%
  • Thermal slack: eliminated (0 under-heat and 0 over-heat)

Interpretation (What this means)

  • Copula scenarios: better capture real winter co-movement (E & T peaks together), so the schedule is less surprised by tail events.
  • Symmetric penalties: stop the optimizer from “running hot” just to avoid under-heat penalties, which reduces wasted fuel and unnecessary cycling.
  • Operational viability: The model remains MILP-compatible and practical for daily scheduling.

Conclusion

This work proposes a robust day-ahead scheduling method for thermal-led microgrids that combines dependence-aware Gaussian copula scenarios with symmetric thermal penalties. The framework improves worst-case economics and reduces battery wear while maintaining thermal adequacy in cold climates.
