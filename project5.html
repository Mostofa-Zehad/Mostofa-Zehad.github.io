71.4 MW Wind Farm Conceptual Design in Binh Thuan Province, Vietnam

This project develops a comprehensive design for a 71.4 MW wind farm using Vestas V150-4.2 MW turbines in Binh Thuan Province, a location known for high wind speeds and low terrain complexity. The project evaluates technical, economic, and infrastructural feasibility, benchmarked against alternate sites in Texas and Connecticut.

1. Introduction & Motivation

With a national goal of 30% renewable energy generation by 2030, Vietnam is rapidly investing in wind power. Binh Thuan, offering a 7.45 m/s mean wind speed and flat terrain, is an ideal candidate for utility-scale onshore deployment.

2. Site Selection & Comparative Analysis

Three sites—Connecticut (CT), Texas City (TX), and Binh Thuan (VN)—were assessed. Vietnam showed the best combination of high power density, simple permitting, and cost efficiency.

3. Turbine Selection & Layout

17 Vestas V150 turbines were arranged in a 6-6-5 layout with spacing based on wind rose data to minimize wake losses.

4. Infrastructure & Wind Resource Monitoring

The site is adjacent to a 110 kV line and existing roads. Two met towers will be installed to measure wind variability across the 12 km² site.

5. Economic Analysis

The total capital expenditure (CAPEX) for the project is estimated at $127.7 million, which includes turbines, balance of plant, infrastructure, and financing costs. This translates to a cost of approximately $1,788 per kW, which is highly competitive compared to global averages for similar onshore wind projects.

The projected net capacity factor (NCF) is 41.6%, reflecting high wind consistency and optimal turbine spacing. With this capacity factor, the annual energy production (AEP) is estimated at around 260,550 MWh. This level of production ensures a strong return on investment and positions the wind farm favorably in Vietnam’s energy mix.

Financial analysis also indicates a Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) between $65–70/MWh, making the project economically attractive for investors and aligning well with regional power purchase agreement rates. The breakdown of CAPEX reveals that the largest cost component is turbine procurement (~52%), followed by balance of plant (~17%) and tower costs (~12%).

6. Risk Management Framework

  • Wind Resource Variability: Mitigated by long-term met tower data collection and CFD-based wind flow modeling.
  • Construction Risks: Managed through detailed geotechnical studies and engaging experienced EPC contractors.
  • Permitting & Regulatory Delays: Addressed by early stakeholder engagement and adherence to IFC guidelines.
  • Wake Effects: Minimized by optimized turbine layout and future yaw control strategies.
  • Financial Fluctuations: Countered by fixed-cost contracts and LCOE sensitivity analysis.
  • Environmental & Social Impacts: Managed through ESIA and transparent community engagement.

7. Conclusion & Path Forward

  • 17 turbines, 71.4 MW capacity
  • NCF of 41.6% → AEP ~260,550 MWh/year
  • LCOE of $65–70/MWh → high regional competitiveness
  • Minimal new infrastructure costs due to existing 110 kV line
  • Next step: detailed feasibility and wind measurement campaigns
