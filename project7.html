Design and Implementation of a Cost-effective Cold Storage Unit Powered by Hybrid Energy Sources

1. Introduction

In Bangladesh, the lack of cold storage facilities in remote agricultural areas contributes heavily to food spoilage and economic loss. This project aims to address this issue by designing a hybrid energy-powered cold storage unit that combines solar, wind, battery, and grid resources to deliver uninterrupted refrigeration with minimized environmental impact and maximum cost-efficiency.

2. Methodology and Load Profile

We analyzed an 800-tonne fish storage (temperature: -18°C to -24°C) and a 40-tonne crop storage (0°C to 4°C), consuming around 459,637 kWh/year. Load profiles were mapped and multiple hybrid configurations simulated using HOMER Pro. The best model minimizes excess energy while achieving ROI within 5 years.

  • Solar PV: 500 kW array
  • Wind Turbine: 1 unit (1.5 kW)
  • Grid Connection: Buy/sell rate set to $0.10/$0.05 per kWh
  • Converter: 372 kW
  • Battery: Optional for smoothing peaks

3. HOMER Pro Optimized Hybrid Model

Using HOMER Pro, the most optimal configuration was identified as a grid-connected system with 500 kW PV, one wind turbine, and a 372 kW converter. This combination results in the highest cost-effectiveness and the lowest excess electricity output (~1.78%).

4. Power Output & Performance

Several hybrid energy models were analyzed:

  • All-PV Off-Grid: High energy surplus (73%) but expensive and inefficient.
  • PV + Wind (Off-Grid): Improved balance but still excessive excess energy (~71%).
  • PV + Wind + Generator: Lower capital cost but high O&M and fuel dependency.
  • Wind + Generator Only: Insufficient generation and excessive number of wind turbines.
  • PV + Grid: Highly efficient with minimal excess but requires vast space.
  • PV + Wind + Grid: Most balanced approach in terms of cost, efficiency, and space.

5. Best Efficient Hybrid Energy Model for the Cold Storage

The most practical and efficient system is a grid-connected hybrid consisting of 250 kW solar PV, 12 units of 1.5 kW wind turbines, and a 185 kW converter. It achieves:

  • Total Energy Generation: 768,877 kWh/year
  • Grid Supply Contribution: ~34.3%
  • Excess Energy: Just 1.4%
  • ROI: ~5 years
  • CO₂ Emission: 13,489 kg/year (much lower than fossil-fuel alternatives)

Economically, this system has a total cost of approximately $341,069, balancing capital investment, replacement, and O&M costs effectively.

6. System Optimization and Automation

To enhance performance, further improvements are recommended:

  • Use of IoT-based energy monitoring for predictive maintenance and real-time load control.
  • Incorporating smart metering and load forecasting to dynamically balance generation and demand.
  • Optimization of component sizing to reduce excess energy generation.
  • Advanced thin-film coatings can reduce internal heat gain, cutting compressor run-time.
  • Remote access control allows adjustments during environmental and demand fluctuations.

These recommendations aim to reduce operational costs, extend system life, and increase energy efficiency—turning the design into a benchmark for future rural energy infrastructure.

7. Conclusion

This hybrid system achieves grid independence up to 75%, rapid ROI, and a scalable model for rural cold chains. By integrating automation and material science, it bridges energy access and food security in developing regions. Further optimization can be achieved through smart metering and IoT-enhanced control systems.
